## Question 1: Information Asymmetry — Does Saudi Arabia Have Private Information That W Lacks?

### Short answer

Yes, but the asymmetry is primarily about Saudi Arabia's **own** spare capacity and cost structure (which determines the value of cooperation for everyone), not about Saudi knowing more about other members' costs. This maps well onto the model's theta, which concerns the **value of cooperation**, not individual types.

### Evidence

#### 1.1 Saudi spare capacity as private information

Saudi Arabia holds approximately **60% of global spare capacity** — roughly 3.0-3.1 million bpd out of OPEC's total of ~5.3 million bpd (IEA/EIA estimates). This spare capacity is the critical variable that determines:

- How credible Saudi threats to flood the market are (the "punishment" option)
- How valuable OPEC cooperation actually is (if Saudi has large spare capacity, a deal is worth more because more production is withheld)
- The true cost of Saudi restraint (which determines what concessions Saudi needs)

Crucially, **the true magnitude of this spare capacity is contested and unverifiable**:

- Saudi Arabia claims a maximum sustainable capacity (MSC) of **12.0 million bpd**, but "it has never produced 12.0 mbd since the discovery of oil in its territories, nor has its claim been tested, and its claim of having a spare capacity of 2.0 mbd has never been substantiated" ([Kingdom Exploration, 2026](https://www.kingdomexploration.com/?page=news&article=opec-spare-capacity-myth-why-5-million-bpd-headlines-hide-a-fragile-reality-dec-09)).

- Independent analysts at **Energy Aspects** and **Rapidan Energy** estimate true deployable spare capacity at just **1.5-2.5 million bpd**, substantially below official claims.

- The EIA itself acknowledges that "both the estimated values and the definitions of capacity from these various sources may be inconsistent," so it makes "its own independent estimates" ([EIA, 2024](https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=66904)).

#### 1.2 The secrecy is documented

Matthew Simmons' *Twilight in the Desert* (2005) documented the systematic secrecy:

> "OPEC countries once routinely reported field-by-field production figures to industry periodicals like the Oil & Gas Journal but stopped doing this when Saudi oil minister Zaki Yamani took office in **1982**."

Since 1982, Saudi Arabia has "maintained reserves data and related technical information as a well-guarded secret, making independent verification of the Kingdom's reserves and resources virtually impossible, leaving various commentators and the world at-large to rely upon speculation and indirect indicators" ([OilPrice.com](https://oilprice.com/Energy/Energy-General/The-Real-Facts-Behind-Saudi-Arabias-Wild-Oil-Claims.html)).

Furthermore: "Saudi Arabia is both able and **incentivized to overrepresent** this spare capacity, as their reporting requirements are opaque and the perception of higher production capacity allows Saudi to exert more influence on both OPEC+ member countries and global oil markets."

#### 1.3 Governance structure insulates private information

"With production controlled solely by Saudi Aramco, a national company largely owned by the state, the strategic policy choice to hold spare capacity is insulated from private shareholder desires for maximum short-term returns" ([Baker Institute](https://www.bakerinstitute.org/research/want-power-oil-market-hold-spare-capacity-not-too-much)). This means Saudi's information is not revealed through capital markets or shareholder reporting the way it might be for a publicly traded firm.

#### 1.4 The 2027 capacity audit as evidence of prior asymmetry

The most striking evidence that information asymmetry existed and mattered comes from **OPEC+'s 2027 quota reform**. In late 2025, OPEC+ approved a new mechanism where a **U.S.-based auditing firm** will independently assess the maximum sustainable capacity (MSC) of 19 of 22 OPEC+ members during 2026, to serve as the basis for 2027 quotas. Saudi Energy Minister Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman called this a "turning point" and described it as "fair and transparent."

The very fact that an independent audit was needed implies that **prior capacity claims were not independently verified** — i.e., information asymmetry existed. The reform was triggered by disputes (Angola's exit in January 2024, UAE's long-running quota grievances) that stemmed precisely from conflicting claims about who could produce how much.

#### 1.5 Mapping to the model

In the model, theta captures the **value of cooperation** — not individual types. Saudi's private information about its own spare capacity directly determines this value:

- **High spare capacity** (theta = high V): Saudi can flood the market, making cooperation extremely valuable (large surplus to divide). Corresponds to V(theta) = r > 1.
- **Low spare capacity** (theta = low V): Saudi's threat is less credible, cooperation is less valuable. Corresponds to V(theta) = 1.

Other members do **not** know Saudi's true spare capacity with certainty. They observe aggregate market data, but the "reality gap" between claimed and effective spare capacity "may exceed 2 million barrels per day." This is precisely the kind of asymmetry the model requires.

### Assessment of evidence strength

**Strong** for the claim that Saudi has private information about the value of cooperation (via spare capacity). The secrecy is well-documented since 1982, the 2027 audit reform is a smoking gun for prior asymmetry, and the governance structure (state-owned Aramco) explains why the information isn't revealed through market mechanisms. The asymmetry is about Saudi's **own** capacity (which determines theta for everyone), not about Saudi knowing other members' costs — which is actually the correct mapping to the model.

## Question 2: Best Single Episode for a Detailed Case Study

### Recommendation: The 1985-86 Price War

This is the best episode for illustrating screening under unanimity. The 2020 episode is a close second but involves Russia (non-OPEC) and pandemic exogeneity that muddies the mechanism. The 1985-86 episode is purely intra-OPEC, shows the full cycle (aggressive offer → rejection → punishment → conservative offer → concessions), and resulted in an explicit institutional bargain (Saudi gets guaranteed 25% share).

### Detailed Chronology

#### Phase 1: The Burden of the Swing Producer (1982-1985)

- **1982**: Saudi Arabia pressed OPEC for audited national production quotas to limit output and boost prices. OPEC assigned Saudi a quota of **4.35 million bpd**.
- **1982-1985**: Saudi Arabia bore the brunt of production cuts to defend the $29/bbl marker price. Its production declined from **10.2 million bpd** (1980) to just **2.3 million bpd** by August 1985 — a loss of nearly 80% of market share.
- Other OPEC members **systematically cheated** on their quotas. Iran and Nigeria were particularly aggressive violators.
- Saudi's revenue collapsed. Fattouh & Mahadeva (2013) note: "the demand for Saudi oil declined from 10.2 mb/d in 1980 to 3.6 mb/d in 1985. This decline in production volumes and loss of market share proved to be very costly."

#### Phase 2: The "Aggressive Offer" — Saudi Demands Compliance (Mid-1985)

- Saudi Arabia, through Oil Minister **Sheikh Ahmed Zaki Yamani**, repeatedly demanded that other members comply with their quotas. This was the "aggressive" offer: "We cut, you cut too; we all maintain discipline."
- This was **rejected** in practice — members verbally agreed but continued to overproduce.
- By mid-1985, Saudi Arabia had formally "terminated its swing producer role" which it had adopted in 1984 alongside the 4.35 million bpd quota, though "in practice it went on performing the swing producer role until **September 1985**."

#### Phase 3: The Punishment — Netback Pricing (September 1985 - July 1986)

- **September 1985**: Saudi Arabia abandoned its swing producer role definitively. Yamani announced Saudi Arabia would no longer unilaterally cut production to support prices.
- **Late 1985**: Saudi adopted **netback pricing** — a revolutionary contract structure where crude oil was priced based on the downstream value of refined products minus refining/transport costs. This guaranteed buyers a profit margin and thus attracted massive new customers.
- **December 1985**: Saudi production surged. Yamani executed "a deft operation involving a rapid build-up of customers on netback-priced contracts."
- **January-July 1986**: Spot prices collapsed from **$28/bbl** (November 1985) to **$8.55/bbl** (July 1986), a 70% decline.
- Total OPEC output surged from 14.1 million bpd (June 1985 low) to **21.8 million bpd** by August 1986.
- The price collapse devastated all members, but **especially** high-cost and fiscally fragile ones (Iran, Nigeria, Venezuela, Ecuador).

#### Phase 4: The "Conservative Offer" Accepted — Geneva and December 1986

- **August 1986**: OPEC oil ministers met in **Geneva** for an emergency session. Iran, desperate for revenue during the Iran-Iraq War, "advanced [a proposal] requiring each member to cut back its crude production in line with the previous 1984 OPEC quota arrangement."
- **October 1986**: Yamani was **dismissed** by King Fahd and replaced by Hisham Nazer. Nazer "ruled out any return to acting as a swing producer."
- **December 1986**: OPEC agreed to a new deal:
  - **Price target**: $18/bbl (vs. $29 pre-crisis) — a major concession by all members
  - **Production ceiling**: 15.8 million bpd (a 7% reduction from August levels)
  - **Saudi's guaranteed share**: ~25% of total OPEC output. "In return for agreeing to an $18 per barrel price objective, Riyadh insisted on a guaranteed quota of approximately 25 percent of total output" ([WRMEA, 1986](https://www.wrmea.org/1986-december/opec-saudi-arabia-gets-tough.html)).
  - Other members agreed to **actually comply** with quotas for the first time — "the 'free-riding' oil exporters that had previously failed to comply with OPEC agreements finally began to limit production to shore up prices."

#### Phase 5: Aftermath (1987)

- "In 1987, the abandonment of the net-backing pricing system and the return to fixed official prices helped to stabilize the market."
- Prices hovered around $18/bbl.
- OPEC total annual revenues recovered to ~$90 billion (a 25% jump from 1986 lows).

### How This Maps to the Model (Screening Interpretation)

| Model Element | 1985-86 Episode |
|---|---|
| **H (hegemon)** | Saudi Arabia |
| **W (weak states)** | Iran, Nigeria, Venezuela, other OPEC members |
| **theta (value of cooperation)** | Saudi's true spare capacity to flood the market |
| **Aggressive signal** | Saudi's switch to netback pricing and production surge |
| **Screening** | Price collapse "screens" W's true willingness to comply — only members genuinely committed to discipline agreed to the December 1986 deal |
| **Conservative equilibrium** | December 1986 deal: $18/bbl target, 15.8 million bpd ceiling |
| **Rent extracted by H** | Guaranteed 25% quota share (up from effective ~14% in mid-1985) |
| **Concessions by W** | Accepted lower price target ($18 vs. $29), agreed to actual quota compliance |
| **Unanimity mechanism** | OPEC's unanimous agreement requirement meant Saudi had to be included in any deal; Saudi's price war demonstrated the cost of exclusion |

### What W Conceded That They Wouldn't Have Under Majority Rule

Under a hypothetical majority rule (where a coalition of, say, Iran + Nigeria + Venezuela + others could set policy without Saudi):
1. They would have set a **higher** price target (closer to $29) to maximize their revenue
2. They would have **excluded Saudi** from the quota allocation (giving themselves larger shares)
3. They would **not** have guaranteed Saudi 25% of output

Under unanimity, Saudi's veto power + demonstrated willingness to punish forced W to:
1. Accept $18/bbl (a massive price concession)
2. Actually comply with quotas (behavioral concession)
3. Guarantee Saudi a 25% share (institutional concession)

## Question 3: Magnitude of Saudi Outside Options (alpha)

### Saudi Arabia's bilateral/unilateral alternatives

#### 3.1 Production cost advantage

Saudi Aramco reported an average cost of crude oil production of **$2.80 per barrel** of oil equivalent in 2018 — the world's lowest. In comparison:

| Producer | Approx. Production Cost ($/bbl) |
|---|---|
| Saudi Arabia (Ghawar) | $5-7 |
| Saudi Arabia (average) | $2.80 |
| Kuwait | $5-10 (median $5.40) |
| Non-OPEC median | $9.70 |
| Norway offshore | $21 |
| US Bakken shale | $51 |

This extreme cost advantage means Saudi Arabia would be profitable at virtually **any** positive oil price, while most competitors would be unprofitable below $40-50/bbl.

Sources: [Asharq Al-Awsat](https://english.aawsat.com/home/article/1993111/aramco%E2%80%99s-oil-production-costs-least-world-28-barrel), [NBER](https://www.nber.org/digest/jan18/limits-opec-output-increase-global-oil-production-costs).

#### 3.2 Fiscal breakeven prices (the relevant constraint)

While production costs are very low, fiscal breakeven prices (the price needed to balance the government budget) are much higher and represent the **operationally relevant** constraint:

| Country | Fiscal Breakeven ($/bbl, 2025 est.) | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Saudi Arabia** | $91-94 (Bloomberg: $111 incl. PIF) | Bloomberg Economics |
| **Saudi Arabia** | $75 (2024 IMF) | IMF REO / FRED |
| **Iraq** | ~$90+ | IMF (among top OPEC states needing high prices) |
| **Iran** | $124 | IMF April 2025 |
| **Kuwait** | $82 | IMF April 2025 |
| **Algeria** | $119 | IMF April 2025 |
| **Bahrain** | $127 | IMF April 2025 |
| **Venezuela** | >$100 (estimated) | Not in IMF MECA tables; indirect evidence |
| **Nigeria** | ~$80-90 (estimated) | IMF deficit projections suggest high breakeven |

**Key insight**: Saudi Arabia's fiscal breakeven (~$91) is **lower** than many members (Iran $124, Algeria $119, Bahrain $127), but **higher** than some (Kuwait $82, Iraq ~$60-90). The critical point for the model is that Saudi can **sustain low prices longer** than most members due to (a) massive sovereign wealth fund (~$900B via PIF + reserves), (b) access to capital markets, and (c) lower fiscal breakeven than the most vulnerable members.

Sources: [FRED — Saudi Breakeven](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/SAUPZPIOILBEGUSD), [Zero Carbon Analytics — IMF Data](https://zerocarbon-analytics.org/insights/briefings/oil-prices-government-finances-in-the-middle-east-and-central-asia/), [CFR — Fiscal Breakeven Prices](https://www.cfr.org/report/fiscal-breakeven-oil-prices).

#### 3.3 Revenue share within OPEC

Saudi Arabia earned **$179 billion** in oil export revenue in 2024, approximately **one-third (33%)** of OPEC's total revenue. Its crude oil and petroleum product exports represented **34%** and **26%** respectively of total OPEC exports in 2023.

Saudi Arabia's production share is also roughly one-third of OPEC output.

Sources: [EIA — OPEC Revenue Fact Sheet](https://www.eia.gov/international/analysis/special-topics/OPEC_Revenues_Fact_Sheet).

#### 3.4 Bilateral deals

Saudi Arabia has been actively building bilateral oil relationships:

- **China**: Oil's share of China's imports from Saudi Arabia has risen from two-thirds a decade ago to **84%** recently. Saudi's trade surplus with China widened from $5-10 billion (2015-16) to **$20-40 billion** in recent years.
- Saudi Arabia has signed long-term supply agreements with **China, India, Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan**.
- Aramco has been expanding downstream investments in China (refinery joint ventures) to lock in demand.

Sources: [S&P Global — Saudi-China Ties](https://www.spglobal.com/en/research-insights/special-reports/saudi-china-ties-and-renminbi-based-oil-trade), [EIA — Saudi Arabia Country Analysis](https://www.eia.gov/international/analysis/country/SAU).

#### 3.5 What if OPEC dissolved?

There is no precise counterfactual estimate, but the academic literature offers guidance:

- In a competitive market, prices would tend toward the marginal cost of the **highest-cost producer needed to meet demand** (roughly $50-70/bbl, depending on shale economics), not Saudi's extraction cost of $3/bbl.
- Saudi would still earn substantial **economic rents** (price minus extraction cost) even without OPEC, due to its position on the cost curve.
- However, **without** OPEC coordination, Saudi could not sustain prices at $80-100+ as it does within the cartel.
- An ECB analysis estimated the impact of OPEC+ production cuts on oil prices at about $3.77-3.85/bbl per cut episode — suggesting OPEC coordination adds a meaningful but modest premium per episode.
- Saudi's revenue would decline significantly but not catastrophically: prices might fall from ~$80 to ~$55-65, and Saudi would increase volume from ~9 million bpd to perhaps 11-12 million bpd, partially offsetting the price decline.

Sources: [NBER — Market Power and OPEC](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w23801/w23801.pdf), [ECB Working Paper](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/pub/pdf/scpwps/ecb.wp2467~c8f35853cc.en.pdf).

#### 3.6 Estimating alpha

In the model, alpha = d_H / V(theta) = (value of Saudi's bilateral/unilateral alternative) / (value of cooperation).

Rough calibration:
- **V(cooperation)**: ~$80-90/bbl price sustained through OPEC coordination, with ~9 million bpd Saudi production = ~$260-290 billion/year.
- **V(unilateral)**: ~$55-65/bbl competitive price, with ~11-12 million bpd = ~$220-280 billion/year.
- **alpha ~ 0.75-0.95**: Saudi's outside option captures a large fraction of the cooperative value, consistent with the model's assumption alpha in (0, 1/r). The key is that alpha is high but strictly less than 1 — Saudi benefits from cooperation but doesn't need it desperately.

**Important caveat**: These are rough estimates. The precise value depends heavily on assumptions about non-OPEC supply response, demand elasticity, and Saudi's true capacity.

## Question 4: OPEC Formation — Unanimity Rule and Founding

### 4.1 The founding story

OPEC was founded on **September 14, 1960** in Baghdad, Iraq, by five founding members: **Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Venezuela**.

The Baghdad Conference (September 10-14, 1960) was convened at the initiative of three key figures:
- **Abdullah al-Tariki** (Saudi Arabia's first Minister of Petroleum and Mineral Resources)
- **Juan Pablo Perez Alfonzo** (Venezuela's oil minister)
- **Abd al-Karim Qasim** (Iraqi Prime Minister, who hosted the conference)

The seed was planted at the **Maadi Yacht Club** in Cairo on the sidelines of the **First Arab Petroleum Congress** (April 1959), where journalist Wanda Jablonski introduced Tariki to Perez Alfonzo. The two signed the **"Maadi Pact"** (or Gentlemen's Agreement), calling for an "Oil Consultation Commission" of exporting countries.

The five "founding fathers" were:
1. Abdullah al-Tariki (Saudi Arabia)
2. Juan Pablo Perez Alfonzo (Venezuela)
3. Dr. Fuad Rouhani (Iran)
4. Dr. Tala'at al-Shaibani (Iraq)
5. Ahmed Sayed Omar (Kuwait)

As one oil historian put it: "Alfonzo provided the rationalization; Tariki, a charismatic speaker, the fire."

Sources: [OPEC — 60 Years since the Maadi Pact](https://www.opec.org/opec_web/en/5387.htm), [GeoExpro — The Road to OPEC 1960](https://geoexpro.com/the-road-to-opec-1960/), [The National — Birth of oil bloc](https://www.thenationalnews.com/business/birth-of-oil-bloc-that-shook-the-world-1.481491).

### 4.2 The unanimity rule

The OPEC Statute (Article 11C) states:

> "Each Full Member Country shall have one vote. **All decisions of the Conference, other than on procedural matters, shall require the unanimous agreement of all Full Members.**"

Additionally, for membership:

> "Any country with a substantial net export of crude petroleum, which has fundamentally similar interests to those of Member Countries, may become a Full Member of the Organization, if accepted by a majority of three-fourths of Full Members, **including the concurring votes of all Founder Members.**"

This gives the five founding members (including Saudi Arabia) a **permanent veto** over new admissions — a stronger protection than even the unanimity rule for regular decisions.

Sources: [OPEC Statute (PDF)](https://www.opec.org/opec_web/static_files_project/media/downloads/publications/OPEC_Statute.pdf), [Fordham International Law Journal](https://ir.lawnet.fordham.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1011&context=ilj).

### 4.3 Was unanimity deliberately chosen by Saudi Arabia?

**The evidence is mixed, and honesty requires saying so.**

**What we know:**
- Unanimity was embedded in the Statute from the founding conference in 1960.
- The design reflects the broader context of **decolonization and sovereignty assertion** — "International law recognizes the sovereignty of states over their natural resources. OPEC's very foundation is rooted in this principle."
- The unanimity rule + one-member-one-vote structure was common in international organizations of this era (e.g., UN General Assembly, many treaty organizations).
- The founding members had roughly comparable production levels in 1960 (Saudi Arabia was not yet the dominant producer it would become — its production was ~1.3 million bpd vs. Iran's ~1.1 million bpd, Kuwait's ~1.7 million bpd, Iraq's ~1.0 million bpd, and Venezuela's ~2.8 million bpd).

**What we do NOT have direct evidence for:**
- A documented instance where Saudi Arabia specifically advocated for unanimity over majority rule.
- Minutes of the Baghdad conference discussing alternative voting mechanisms.
- Evidence that Saudi Arabia "chose" unanimity strategically in the way the model's Stage 0 envisions.

**Most likely interpretation**: Unanimity was a **consensus choice** among the founding members, driven by sovereignty concerns (no country wanted to be bound by a majority). Saudi Arabia was a co-architect of OPEC (via Tariki), but unanimity was not a unilateral Saudi design choice — it reflected the collective preference of all five founding members.

**Implications for the model**: Framing **(b)** — "Given unanimity, H extracts screening rents" — is better supported than framing **(a)** — "H chose unanimity." However, the model's prediction that unanimity benefits H more than majority rule is validated by the **observed behavior**: Saudi Arabia has consistently used its veto power and threat capacity within the unanimity framework to extract disproportionate gains.

### 4.4 Power distribution at founding

In 1960, production levels were:
| Country | 1960 Production (approx.) |
|---|---|
| Venezuela | 2.8 million bpd |
| Kuwait | 1.7 million bpd |
| Saudi Arabia | 1.3 million bpd |
| Iran | 1.1 million bpd |
| Iraq | 1.0 million bpd |

Venezuela was actually the largest producer at founding. Saudi Arabia's dominance emerged **later** (1970s-1980s) as it massively expanded capacity. This matters: Saudi Arabia may not have been "H" at the founding moment, but became H over time as its spare capacity and production grew.

Sources: [OPEC — Brief History](https://www.opec.org/opec_web/en/about_us/24.htm), [EBSCO — OPEC Founded](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/opec-founded), [Britannica — OPEC](https://www.britannica.com/topic/OPEC).

## Question 5: Evidence That Saudi Arabia Extracted Rents (Disproportionate Share)

### 5.1 The 1986 quota guarantee

The clearest evidence of rent extraction comes from the 1985-86 price war resolution:

> "In return for agreeing to an $18 per barrel price objective, **Riyadh insisted on a guaranteed quota of approximately 25 percent of total output**." ([WRMEA, 1986](https://www.wrmea.org/1986-december/opec-saudi-arabia-gets-tough.html))

This was explicitly a **disproportionate** share — Saudi had been producing as little as 14% of OPEC output in mid-1985. The guaranteed 25% share was a direct institutional concession extracted through the price war.

### 5.2 The swing producer "burden" as rent extraction

The conventional narrative frames Saudi Arabia's swing producer role as a **burden** (Saudi cuts while others cheat). But this narrative misses the strategic logic:

- By accepting the swing role, Saudi Arabia obtained **de facto control** over OPEC pricing — it determined the effective price band by choosing how much to cut or add.
- Saudi's **overproduction** averaged only 3.2% above quota (1995-2007), compared to Qatar's 18.5% — suggesting Saudi was more disciplined, but also that its quota was set closer to its preferred level ([Molchanov, Duke working paper](https://sites.duke.edu/djepapers/files/2016/08/paveldje.pdf)).
- The swing role gave Saudi the **option value** of spare capacity: "the flexibility provided by spare capacity has significant financial value (what economists call option value)" ([Baker Institute](https://www.bakerinstitute.org/research/want-power-oil-market-hold-spare-capacity-not-too-much)).

### 5.3 The 45% voluntary cut burden

Currently, Saudi Arabia **alone bears approximately 45% of total voluntary OPEC+ cuts**, meaning a sacrifice of significant oil revenues in favor of price stability. This appears to be a cost, but:

- Saudi benefits disproportionately from **price stability** because it has the largest absolute production volume
- The voluntary cuts are Saudi's **choice** (not imposed), indicating Saudi prefers this arrangement to the alternative
- Saudi's fiscal breakeven ($91/bbl) means it benefits enormously from prices sustained above $80

### 5.4 The 2024 Angola exit and 2026 UAE exit: evidence of grievances

**Angola** left OPEC in January 2024 after its quota was cut by 350,000 bpd (from 1.46 to 1.11 million bpd). Angola's oil minister stated that **"OPEC was not serving the country's interests"** ([Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2023/12/21/angola-to-leave-opec-over-disagreement-on-oil-production-quotas)). The departure reflected broader African grievances about declining influence and unfair quota allocation.

**The UAE** announced on April 28, 2026 (yesterday) that it will leave OPEC effective May 1. The core dispute: "The UAE has long pushed for higher OPEC production quotas as it sought to expand capacity well beyond the levels assigned to it by the cartel. OPEC quotas had most recently limited the UAE to 3.2 million barrels of production a day, when in fact it has capacity to produce closer to 5 million barrels a day" ([CNN](https://edition.cnn.com/2026/04/28/business/uae-quits-opec-in-major-blow-to-oil-cartel-and-saudi-arabia)). Crucially, "the UAE and Saudi Arabia have increasingly competed over economic issues."

These exits are evidence that **members felt the quota system was unfair** — i.e., that some members (particularly Saudi Arabia) were extracting rents at others' expense. The old quota system's "patchwork of historical baselines" had "long been criticized as misaligned with actual capability — especially as Gulf producers such as the UAE and Saudi Arabia have invested heavily to expand capacity while some African and smaller producers have seen structural declines" ([IndexBox](https://www.indexbox.io/blog/opec-approves-new-2027-production-quota-mechanism-based-on-capacity-audits/)).

### 5.5 Academic evidence on rent distribution

Direct academic estimates of the distribution of OPEC rents across members are surprisingly scarce. The closest relevant literature:

- **Almoguera, Douglas & Herrera (2011)**, "Testing for the cartel in OPEC: non-cooperative collusion or just non-cooperative?" — finds periods of cooperation and non-cooperation, but does not estimate rent distribution across members.
- **Fattouh & Mahadeva (2013)**, "OPEC: What Difference Has It Made?" — focuses on whether OPEC raises prices above competitive levels, not on within-OPEC distribution.
- **NBER Working Paper 23801** — examines market power and production misallocation in OPEC, finding that low-cost producers (especially Saudi Arabia) are under-producing relative to efficient allocation, implying that OPEC's quota system constrains Saudi's efficient output, but the price premium this creates benefits all members (though disproportionately benefits the largest producer).
- The literature on OPEC as a cartel generally treats it as a **dominant firm + competitive fringe** model where Saudi Arabia is the dominant firm, which inherently implies Saudi extracts leader's rents.

### 5.6 The 2027 quota reform as evidence

The 2027 capacity-based quota reform is perhaps the strongest indirect evidence that the **old system** gave Saudi disproportionate rents:

- The old system used "historical baselines" that reflected past negotiations, not actual capacity — giving Saudi Arabia (which negotiated its 25% share from a position of strength in 1986) an embedded advantage.
- The new system ties quotas to audited capacity, which would "reward countries that invest in upstream projects" — implicitly acknowledging that the old system did not.
- Members like UAE and Angola who complained about unfair quotas were essentially arguing that Saudi Arabia's historical baseline was too generous relative to its actual capacity utilization.

### Assessment of evidence strength

**Moderate to strong**. The 1986 guaranteed 25% share is direct evidence of disproportionate institutional gains. The Angola exit (2024) and UAE exit (2026) provide strong indirect evidence that members perceived the system as unfair. Academic literature supports the dominant-firm interpretation. However, there is no single academic paper that directly estimates "Saudi Arabia received X% more rent than its production share would suggest" — this gap could be an opportunity for the paper's contribution.
