## 1. OPEC's Decision-Making Rule

### 1.1 Formal Statutory Provisions

The OPEC Statute establishes **unanimity** as the decision-making rule. Article 11 of the Statute provides:

> "Each Full Member Country shall have one vote. All decisions of the Conference, other than on procedural matters, shall require the unanimous agreement of all Full Members."

The Statute was adopted by a unanimous decision of the Founder Members under Resolution II.6 at its second meeting (January 1961, Caracas). OPEC operates on the principles of **unanimity and "one member, one vote,"** with each country paying an equal membership fee.

**Sources:**
- [OPEC Statute (official PDF)](https://www.opec.org/opec_web/static_files_project/media/downloads/publications/OPEC_Statute.pdf)
- [OPEC Statute page](https://www.opec.org/opec_web/en/publications/345.htm)
- [OPEC Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC)

### 1.2 Consensus vs. Unanimity in Practice

The formal rule is unanimity, but practice operates closer to consensus (absence of formal objection). Key features:

- **Resolutions become effective after 30 days** from the conclusion of the meeting, unless the Secretariat receives notification to the contrary from a Member Country.
- **Absent members** are bound unless they object within 10 days before the publication date.
- **Production quotas are adopted unanimously** rather than by majority, but are **not legally binding** -- members can and do deviate.
- Decisions require consensus-building, which "typically induces rumors and leaks that affect the oil market before the concluding resolution" (Oxford Academic, *Journal of World Energy Law & Business*, 2024).

**Source:** [What drives OPEC production policy?](https://academic.oup.com/jwelb/article/17/1/19/7497082)

### 1.3 What Happens When a Member Disagrees?

Members can effectively **block or exit**:

- **Angola (December 2023)**: Left OPEC after disagreeing with production quotas. Oil Minister Diamantino de Azevedo stated Angola "does not gain anything by remaining in the organization" and that the cartel's quota system "no longer aligns with the country's values and interests." Angola's quota had been cut by 350,000 b/d -- from 1.46M to 1.11M b/d -- and the country refused to accept it.
- **Ecuador** withdrew in 2020, **Qatar** in 2019 -- both over disagreements with OPEC policy.
- **Nigeria** expressed discontent over quotas at the November 2023 meeting but did not exit.

**Key implication for the model**: The unanimity rule gives every member a formal veto, but in practice the threat of Saudi retaliation (flooding the market) disciplines members into accepting outcomes they dislike. The "exit option" (Angola) demonstrates the outside option at work -- members who cannot be screened prefer to leave rather than accept unfavorable allocations.

**Sources:**

- [Angola Exits OPEC After Production Quota Dispute](https://jpt.spe.org/angola-exits-opec-after-production-quota-dispute)

### 1.4 Model Mapping Assessment

The OPEC-model mapping on decision rules is **strong**:

| One member, one vote | Confirmed -- regardless of production volume |

## 2. Saudi Spare Capacity as Private Information

### 2.1 The Secrecy of Saudi Reserves and Capacity Data

Saudi Arabia's true spare production capacity has been one of the most closely guarded secrets in global energy markets.

**Historical secrecy**:
- Saudi Arabia maintained reserves data and technical information as a **well-guarded secret** since at least 1982.
- Saudi Aramco **repeatedly and consistently reported oil reserves of ~260-261 billion barrels every year from the mid-1980s** through 2019, without revision, despite rising production, price swings, and technological changes.
- **No independent audit** of Saudi reserves was conducted until **2019**, when DeGolyer & MacNaughton was engaged ahead of the Aramco IPO. The audit found reserves of 268.5 billion barrels -- broadly consistent with Saudi claims but representing the **first time external verification** had occurred.

**Sources:**
- [Oil reserves in Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_reserves_in_Saudi_Arabia)
- [Saudi Arabia has always claimed vast oil reserves. An audit just proved it's right](https://www.cnn.com/2019/01/09/business/saudi-arabia-oil-gas-reserves/index.html)
- [Aramco audit to uncover Saudi oil reserve secrets](https://www.newarab.com/News/2016/11/17/Aramco-audit-to-uncover-Saudi-oil-reserve-secrets)

### 2.2 The Massive Disagreement Between Agencies

The divergence in spare capacity estimates is strikingly large:

| Source | Saudi spare capacity estimate | Date |
|---|---|---|
| **IEA** | 3.1 million b/d (of 5.3M total OPEC) | 2025 |
| **IEA (revised)** | 2.43 million b/d | Late 2025 |
| **Reuters analysis** | 600,000 to 1 million b/d | 2025 |
| **Aramco CEO** | MSC of 12 million b/d sustained for 1 year | October 2025 |
| **Industry executives (private)** | Doubt Saudi can produce 11-11.5M b/d for any lengthy period | Various |

The cumulative **"reality gap"** between claimed and effective spare capacity may exceed **2 million barrels per day** -- roughly a 40-80% discrepancy.

**EIA methodology change (December 2025)**: The EIA updated its definitions to distinguish between:
- **Maximum sustainable capacity**: theoretical maximum assuming full utilization and no disruptions
- **Effective production capacity**: what can actually be reached within 90 days and sustained using sound practices

This methodological shift **implicitly acknowledged** what contrarian analysts had argued for years: much of OPEC's reported spare capacity exists only on paper.

### 2.3 Matthew Simmons and "Twilight in the Desert"

Matthew Simmons (2005) published *Twilight in the Desert: The Coming Saudi Oil Shock and the World Economy* (Wiley), which systematically challenged Saudi capacity claims:

- Simmons analyzed **200 Society of Petroleum Engineers technical papers** on Saudi fields.
- He concluded that "the oil extraction techniques being used there were techniques that one might use if the fields were quite depleted."
- His verdict: "there is only a small probability that Saudi Arabia will ever deliver the quantities of petroleum that are assigned to it in all the major forecasts."
- He also noted it was "widely believed that most, if not all, OPEC members exaggerated the sizes of their reserves in order to have the largest possible quota."

While some of Simmons' peak-oil predictions did not materialize, his core point -- that **Saudi capacity data is opaque and strategically managed** -- remains valid and widely acknowledged.

**Source:** [Twilight in the Desert (Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twilight_in_the_Desert)

### 2.4 The 1980s Reserve Inflation Episode

In the mid-1980s, multiple OPEC members dramatically **inflated reported reserves** with no corresponding discoveries:

- Because quotas were partially based on proven reserves, members had incentives to exaggerate.
- Iraq's reserves jumped more than **threefold** in a single revision.
- Saudi Arabia uplifted reserves by approximately 50%, reportedly after "much technical work."
- Kuwait, UAE, and Venezuela also made large upward revisions.
- **No significant new discoveries** justified these increases.

This episode directly demonstrates **strategic manipulation of private information** within OPEC -- members inflating type reports to obtain more favorable allocations.

**Source:** [OPEC Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OPEC)

### 2.5 The Abqaiq Attack (September 2019) as Information Revelation

The drone attack on Abqaiq and Khurais facilities on September 14, 2019 served as an **involuntary information revelation event**:

- Production dropped from 9.8M to 4.1M b/d overnight -- a loss of **5.7M b/d** (about 5% of global production).
- The attack struck the processing facility handling more than **double** the total global spare capacity.
- It revealed the **concentration risk** and the vulnerability of Saudi infrastructure.
- Speed of recovery provided partial information about actual spare capacity.

**Model mapping**: The Abqaiq attack can be interpreted as a shock that partially revealed theta -- Saudi's true capacity -- to the world, temporarily reducing information asymmetry.

**Sources:**

- [After Saudi attack, spare capacity concerns linger: EIA](https://www.spglobal.com/platts/en/market-insights/latest-news/oil/100819-after-saudi-attack-spare-capacity-concerns-linger-eia)

### 2.6 Strategic Ambiguity Quotes

- **Ali Al-Naimi** (Saudi Oil Minister, 2015): "No one can set the price of oil -- it's up to Allah." While directed at prices, this reflects the kingdom's posture of **strategic opacity**.
- Saudi Arabia **committed to maintain spare oil output capacity** for price and market stability (Naimi, Bloomberg 2011) but never disclosed exact figures.
- "Any signals of changes in crude oil production from Saudi Arabia...often send ripples through the market" -- the market's **uncertainty about when and how much capacity will be deployed** represents a form of power in itself.

## 3. Historical Episodes of Screening

### 3.1 The 1985-86 Price War: Punishing Quota Cheaters

**Background**: From 1980 to 1985, Saudi Arabia played "swing producer," cutting its own production from ~10M b/d to just **2M b/d** to support OPEC prices. Other members systematically cheated on their quotas while Saudi Arabia bore the adjustment cost alone.

**The breaking point**: By mid-1985, Saudi profits had fallen below Cournot profits (Griffin 1994). Sheikh Yamani had **repeatedly warned** other OPEC members at Geneva meetings (1982-1985) that Saudi Arabia would expand output unless cheating stopped. His warnings went unheeded.

**The flood**: In late 1985, Yamani launched netback pricing -- guaranteeing refiners a margin -- and Saudi production surged from **2M to 10M b/d in four months**. Prices crashed from **$28/barrel to $8.55** between November 1985 and July 1986.

**Aftermath**: In September 1986, Yamani gave an "hour-long speech at the Kennedy School where he decried the state of negotiations, opined that his warnings about the glut had gone unheeded." He was fired by King Fahd shortly after. OPEC eventually re-established quota discipline.

**Model mapping**:
- This maps to **H rejecting an aggressive offer**. The other OPEC members (W) were making "aggressive" proposals -- cheating on quotas while expecting Saudi Arabia to continue absorbing cuts.
- Saudi Arabia's **type** (high spare capacity theta) was revealed when it flooded the market, demonstrating it could sustain 10M b/d.
- The episode established **credibility** for future screening: after 1986, other members knew Saudi Arabia would follow through on threats.
- Griffin (1994) interprets this as Saudi Arabia adopting a **tit-for-tat** punishment strategy after abandoning the swing producer role.

**Sources:**
- [THE 1985-86 OIL PRICE COLLAPSE: What Does Game Theory Add? (Griffin 1994)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1465-7295.1994.tb01350.x)
- [The Swing Producer: Minister Yamani](https://cafe.com/article/the-swing-producer-oil-minister-yamani-and-the-unique-risks-of-oil-gluts/)

### 3.2 The 2014-16 Price War: Market Share Strategy

**Background**: In November 2014, OPEC met in Vienna and decided **not to reduce supply** despite falling prices. Saudi Oil Minister Ali Naimi stated: "If I reduce [production], what happens to my market share? The price will go up and the Russians, the Brazilians, U.S. shale oil producers will take my share."

**The strategy**: Saudi Arabia pushed OPEC to **defend market share** rather than defend prices. This was widely interpreted as targeting US shale producers, but it also disciplined smaller OPEC members:
- The strategy "transfers the burden of adjustment onto other producers, both within OPEC and outside of it, including countries that in the past took advantage of Saudi Arabia's willingness to manage the oil market."
- In December 2015, OPEC reiterated its commitment to a "market-share" strategy.
- Prices fell from ~$110/barrel to below $30/barrel.

**Model mapping**:
- This maps to **Saudi Arabia revealing its type as high-capacity**. By refusing to cut and maintaining high production, Saudi Arabia demonstrated it could absorb low prices that devastated members with higher production costs (Venezuela, Nigeria, Algeria).
- Smaller members were forced to accept the market-share strategy because they **could not credibly threaten** Saudi Arabia -- they lacked spare capacity and fiscal reserves.
- The episode reduced other members' surplus: Venezuela's economy collapsed, Nigeria entered recession.

- [Oil Shock - Decoding the Causes and Consequences (CIRSD)](https://www.cirsd.org/en/horizons/horizons-spring-2015--issue-no3/oil-shock-%E2%80%94-decoding-the-causes-and-consequences-of-the-2014-oil-price-drop)

### 3.3 The 2020 Saudi-Russia Price War

**Background**: On March 5, 2020, OPEC agreed to cut production by 1.5M b/d through Q2 2020 in response to COVID demand destruction. On **March 6, Russia refused** to participate.

**Saudi retaliation**: On March 8, Saudi Arabia announced **price discounts of $6-8/barrel** to US, European, and Asian customers. On March 10, Saudi Arabia announced it would increase production from 9.7M to **12.3M b/d** from April 2020.

**Market impact**: Oil prices fell from ~$50/barrel to ~$10/barrel. WTI briefly went **negative** in April 2020.

**Resolution**: The OPEC+ deal of April 12, 2020 cut 9.7M b/d -- the largest coordinated cut in history.

**Model mapping**:
- This is the clearest example of **Saudi Arabia using its spare capacity as a punishment device** against a partner who "rejected the offer."
- Russia is not a typical W in the model (it's a powerful non-OPEC partner), but the mechanism is identical: when the other party refuses to accept the proposed allocation, H floods the market.
- **The resolution required Russia to concede** -- accepting large production cuts -- demonstrating the screening mechanism at work.

### 3.4 The 2025-26 Punishment of Quota Cheaters

**Background**: OPEC+ production compliance averaged only **67%** in Q1 2025, with overproduction totaling **1.2M b/d** -- Iraq, Kazakhstan, and Russia accounting for 890,000 b/d of the excess.

**Saudi response**:
- **April 2025**: Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman warned that **"free riders will face consequences."**
- **June 2025**: He escalated, threatening Riyadh might **"abandon its role as the lone wolf stabilizing markets"** unless compliance improved.
- Saudi Arabia reversed 40% of its voluntary 1M b/d production cut, adding 400,000 b/d between May and August 2025.
- By July 2025, Saudi Arabia **raised production above its OPEC+ quota** in a rare breach -- intentionally flooding the market to punish cheaters.

**Key quote**: "Comply, or drown in cheap oil" -- widely reported characterization of Saudi strategy.

**Model mapping**: This is **screening in real time**. Saudi Arabia is using its spare capacity (theta) to discipline members who deviate from the agreed allocation. The punishment mechanism -- flood the market -- is credible precisely because other members **do not know Saudi Arabia's true willingness** to sustain low prices.

### 3.5 The 2027 Quota Reform: Third-Party Capacity Audits

**The reform**: In November 2025, OPEC+ approved a mechanism to **assess maximum sustainable production capacity** (MSC) for 2027 baselines:

- **DeGolyer & MacNaughton** (US consultancy) will audit MSC for **19 countries** between January and September 2026.
- Separate arrangements for Russia and Venezuela; Iran will use domestic production figures.
- MSC defined as: **output a country can sustain for one year and bring onstream within 90 days.**
- Future quotas will be **tied to audited capacity**, replacing a "patchwork of historical baselines."
- Assessments will be **annual** going forward.

**Why this matters for the model**: The capacity audit is an **attempt to reduce information asymmetry**. It directly addresses the problem that capacity data has been private information. However:
- Saudi Arabia and UAE have **invested heavily to expand capacity** and would benefit from capacity-based quotas.
- African and smaller producers have seen **structural declines** and would receive lower baselines.
- The reform **shifts rents toward high-capacity producers** -- consistent with the model's prediction that information revelation benefits H when theta is high.

**Model implication**: The audit can be interpreted as a partial shift from screening (where W must offer conservatively due to uncertainty about theta) toward a revealed-type regime. If successful, it would reduce the screening rent but increase allocation efficiency.

- [OPEC+ Members to Undergo Annual Oil Capacity Audit](https://money.usnews.com/investing/news/articles/2025-12-02/opec-members-to-undergo-annual-oil-capacity-audit-under-new-plan-sources-say)

### 3.6 Evidence of Conservative Offers

**Direct evidence** that smaller members made conservative concessions due to fear of Saudi retaliation:

1. **Post-1986 compliance**: After the 1985-86 price war, OPEC members substantially improved quota compliance. The **credible threat** had been established.

2. **2020 resolution**: After the Saudi-Russia price war, Russia accepted production cuts of ~2.5M b/d -- far larger than initially proposed. The threat was credible because Saudi Arabia had already demonstrated willingness to increase production to 12.3M b/d.

3. **UAE concessions (2021)**: "The UAE pledged to make additional voluntary output cuts at the request of Saudi Arabia, which wanted to share the burden of cuts in an effort to support prices." The UAE -- a wealthy, capable producer -- still deferred to Saudi preferences.

4. **2025 compensation mechanism**: Iraq, UAE, Kazakhstan, and Oman were required to **compensate for overproduction** from December 2025 through June 2026 -- effectively paying a penalty. They accepted because the alternative (Saudi flooding) was worse.

5. **Angola exit as outside option**: Angola's exit demonstrates that the screening mechanism imposes real costs on W. Members who cannot afford the "conservative offer" (lower quota) exercise the exit option rather than stay and be screened.

## 4. Academic Literature on OPEC as a Cartel with Private Information

### 4.1 Foundational Models

**Hnyilicza & Pindyck (1976)** -- "Pricing Policies for a Two-Part Exhaustible Resource Cartel: The Case of OPEC" (*European Economic Review*):
- Divides OPEC into "saver" nations (low discount rates, led by Gulf states) and "spender" nations (high discount rates, need immediate revenue).
- Models Nash bargaining between the two groups.
- Discusses "bang-bang" solutions and the "considerable temptation to cheat."
- **Relevance**: Identifies the fundamental heterogeneity within OPEC but does not model private information per se.

**Griffin (1985, AER)** -- "OPEC Behavior: A Test of Alternative Hypotheses":
- Tests cartel, competitive, target revenue, and swing producer models.
- Finds evidence supporting partial cartel with Saudi Arabia as swing producer (1983-85).
- When Saudi profits fell below Cournot level in 1985, it abandoned the swing role.

**Griffin & Neilson (1994, Economic Inquiry)** -- "The 1985-86 Oil Price Collapse and Afterwards: What Does Game Theory Add?":
- Interprets the 1985-86 episode through game theory: Saudi adopted **tit-for-tat punishment** strategy.
- Evidence of swing producer strategy 1983-85, followed by Cournot reversion, then tit-for-tat.

**Sources:**
- [Griffin 1994](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1465-7295.1994.tb01350.x)
- [Hnyilicza & Pindyck 1976](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/001429217690009X)

### 4.2 Saudi Arabia as Dominant Producer

**Alhajji & Huettner (2000a, Energy Journal)** -- "OPEC and World Crude Oil Markets from 1973 to 1994: Cartel, Oligopoly, or Competitive?":
- Tests whether OPEC, OPEC core, or Saudi Arabia fit competitive, Cournot, or dominant firm models.
- **Results reject all models except the dominant firm model for Saudi Arabia**.
- Neither OPEC as a whole nor the OPEC core can be characterized as a dominant producer -- only Saudi Arabia.

**Alhajji & Huettner (2000b, Energy Journal)** -- "The Target Revenue Model and the World Oil Market":
- Tests the target revenue hypothesis against oil market data 1971-1994.
- Related to the question of what drives Saudi pricing decisions.

**Nakov & Nuno (2013, Economic Journal)** -- "Saudi Arabia and the Oil Market":
- Models Saudi Arabia as a **Stackelberg dominant supplier** that anticipates behavior of fringe producers and oil consumers.
- Saudi Arabia "systematically restricts its production" with spare capacity much larger than the rest of the world's producers combined.
- Its production is "quite volatile despite the Kingdom witnessing few domestic shocks."
- Saudi Arabia produces less than capacity to charge a **high markup over marginal cost**.

**Smith (2005)**: Found **no evidence** supporting the dominant producer theory, concluding that if Saudi Arabia assumed a Stackelberg leader role, "it wasn't pursued with enough vigor and continuity to leave a discernible pattern in the data."

**Sources:**
- [Alhajji & Huettner 2000a](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.5547/ISSN0195-6574-EJ-Vol21-No3-2)
- [Nakov & Nuno 2013](https://www.galonuno.com/uploads/1/3/4/6/134687692/saudi_arabia.pdf)

### 4.3 Quota Cheating and Enforcement

**Lund (1992, Journal of Law and Economics)** -- "The Incentive to Cheat: An Empirical Analysis of OPEC":
- Analyzes incentives for quota violation.
- Higher oil prices **increase the payoff from cheating**, making enforcement more difficult during high-price periods.

**Key structural finding**: OPEC lacks any formal enforcement mechanism. There is no "global oil police force" capable of punishing violators. The **only enforcement mechanism is Saudi Arabia's threat to flood the market** -- which is costly to Saudi Arabia itself.

**The prisoner's dilemma**: While collectively restricting supply benefits all members, individually it makes more sense for each to increase production. This classic cartel instability is exacerbated by:
- Difficulty of objectively measuring production
- Different discount rates across members
- Capacity constraints and different cost structures
- Different fiscal break-even prices

**Sources:**
- [The Incentive to Cheat](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/abs/10.1086/467374)
- [Fattouh & Mahadeva 2013](https://www.oxfordenergy.org/wpcms/wp-content/uploads/2013/01/MEP-3.pdf)

### 4.4 Comprehensive Surveys

**Fattouh & Mahadeva (2013, Annual Review of Resource Economics)** -- "OPEC: What Difference Has It Made?":
- Reviews the evolution of OPEC models.
- Main conclusion: OPEC's pricing power varies over time.
- OPEC's "fragile organization structure" and lack of formal enforcement are key themes.
- Identifies Saudi Arabia's spare capacity as the critical enforcement tool.

**Source:** [Fattouh & Mahadeva 2013](https://ideas.repec.org/a/anr/reseco/v5y2013p427-443.html)

### 4.5 Literature Gap

**Notable absence**: There does not appear to be a published paper that models OPEC explicitly through the lens of **Bayesian persuasion** or **screening with private information about capacity type**. The existing literature focuses on:
- Repeated game models (prisoners' dilemma, tit-for-tat)
- Dominant firm / competitive fringe models
- Nash bargaining between groups (savers vs. spenders)

**Your model's contribution**: A model where Saudi Arabia's private information about spare capacity (theta) creates a screening mechanism under unanimity would be **novel in the OPEC literature**. The existing models treat capacity as observable or focus on cheating incentives rather than on how **uncertainty about capacity** creates rents for the informed party.

## 6. Honest Limitations of the OPEC-Model Mapping

### 6.1 Where the Mapping Works Well

| Model feature | OPEC fit | Strength |
|---|---|---|
| Unanimity/consensus rule | Article 11 requires unanimous agreement | **Strong** |
| Hegemon with private info | Saudi spare capacity is genuinely private | **Strong** |
| Outside option (alpha) | Saudi can flood market unilaterally | **Strong** |
| Screening mechanism | Smaller members concede due to uncertainty | **Moderate-Strong** |
| Conservative offers | Post-1986 compliance improvement; 2020 concessions | **Moderate** |
| Entry decision | Angola exit = outside option; joining OPEC = entering coalition | **Moderate** |

### 6.2 Where the Mapping Breaks Down or Is Strained

1. **Cooperation surplus vs. supply restriction**: The formal model is about dividing a cooperation surplus V(theta). In OPEC, the "surplus" comes from **restricting supply** to raise prices above the competitive level. This is an important structural difference:
   - In the model, cooperation creates value (V > 0).
   - In OPEC, cooperation creates value **only through market power** -- restricting supply.
   - The "pie" (total OPEC rents from above-competitive pricing) depends on collective action, which maps reasonably to V(theta), but the mechanism is supply restriction rather than positive-sum cooperation.

2. **Saudi doesn't always benefit from consensus**: The model predicts H prefers unanimity. But:
   - Saudi Arabia has sometimes **acted unilaterally** (1985-86, 2020, 2025) -- suggesting it **prefers** to act outside the consensus framework at times.
   - The **swing producer role** means Saudi sometimes bears disproportionate costs of cooperation (cutting production while others cheat).
   - When Saudi acts unilaterally (flooding), it is **destroying the cooperation surplus** to punish defectors -- consistent with the model's threat but not with the equilibrium where unanimity is always preferred.
   - **However**: Saudi Arabia consistently returns to the consensus framework after punishment episodes, suggesting it **does** ultimately prefer unanimity as an institution. The punishment phases are off-equilibrium paths that sustain the consensus equilibrium.

3. **Multiple Ws with different types**: In OPEC, the Ws are highly heterogeneous:
   - Venezuela, Nigeria, Algeria = high fiscal break-even, low spare capacity
   - UAE, Kuwait = low fiscal break-even, moderate spare capacity
   - Iraq, Iran = large capacity but political constraints
   - The model has homogeneous Ws, which misses important within-W dynamics.

4. **Repeated game dynamics**: The model is a one-shot (or two-round) game. OPEC is a **repeated interaction** where:
   - Reputation effects matter (the 1986 episode created lasting credibility)
   - Punishment strategies evolve (tit-for-tat, then graduated responses)
   - The discount factor plays a different role than in Baron-Ferejohn

5. **OPEC quotas are not binding**: The model assumes the agreed allocation is implemented. In OPEC, quota cheating is endemic -- compliance averaged only 67% in Q1 2025. The **implementation gap** is substantial.

6. **Saudi's private information is not just about capacity**: theta in the model is Saudi's type (spare capacity). In reality, Saudi's private information includes:
   - True spare capacity (how much can they produce?)
   - Fiscal break-even price (how low can they tolerate prices?)
   - Political willingness to sustain low prices (domestic political constraints)
   - Investment plans (future capacity changes)
   - All of these contribute to the screening mechanism, but the model captures only the capacity dimension.

7. **The "pie" is not exogenous**: In the model, V(theta) is drawn by nature. In OPEC, the size of the pie (total rents from price markup) depends on:
   - Global demand (macro conditions)
   - Non-OPEC supply (shale, renewables)
   - Compliance by all members
   - This endogeneity is not captured by the model.

### 6.3 Strongest Elements for a Discussion Section

If using OPEC as a case study in the Discussion, the **strongest** elements to emphasize are:

1. **Spare capacity as genuinely private information**: The IEA/EIA/Reuters disagreement (2.43M vs. 600K-1M b/d) provides hard numbers for the information asymmetry.

2. **The unanimity rule is statutory**: Article 11 provides a clean institutional mapping.

3. **The punishment mechanism is well-documented**: 1986, 2014, 2020, 2025 -- four episodes where Saudi Arabia used spare capacity to discipline members.

4. **The 2027 capacity audit is an attempt to reduce information asymmetry**: The DeGolyer & MacNaughton third-party audit directly addresses the private information problem, confirming that the problem is real and recognized.

5. **Saudi Arabia does seem to benefit from the consensus framework**: Despite occasional unilateral episodes, Saudi Arabia consistently returns to and advocates for consensus ("Consensus building is an art" -- Prince Abdulaziz bin Salman).

## 8. Summary Assessment

### Fit with the formal model

The OPEC case provides **strong support** for the model's core mechanism:

1. **Private information exists**: Saudi spare capacity is genuinely unknown, with expert estimates diverging by 40-80%.
2. **Unanimity rule is formal**: OPEC Statute Article 11.
3. **Screening operates**: Smaller members accept unfavorable quotas because they cannot assess Saudi's true type -- if they push too hard, Saudi floods the market (1986, 2014, 2020, 2025).
4. **Outside option matters**: Saudi can and does act unilaterally (bilateral deals with China/India, unilateral production increases).
5. **Conservative offers documented**: Post-punishment compliance improvements; UAE voluntary concessions; 2020 Russia capitulation.

### Key limitations to acknowledge

1. OPEC's "surplus" is from supply restriction, not positive-sum cooperation.
2. Saudi doesn't always prefer consensus -- it sometimes prefers unilateral action.
3. The repeated game structure matters (reputation, evolving strategies).
4. Heterogeneous Ws with different cost structures.
5. Quota non-compliance is endemic (67% compliance in 2025).

### Recommendation

OPEC works as a **compelling illustrative case** in the Discussion section, but should be presented with appropriate caveats. The strongest framing: "OPEC illustrates how private information about capacity creates informational rents under consensus. The model's core mechanism -- that uncertainty about theta forces conservative offers from uninformed parties -- maps onto documented episodes where smaller OPEC members accepted unfavorable quotas due to uncertainty about Saudi Arabia's true spare capacity and willingness to flood the market."
