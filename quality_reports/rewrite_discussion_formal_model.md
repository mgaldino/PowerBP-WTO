# Discussion reescrita: The Hegemon's Choice of Consensus

## Why a hegemon may choose consensus

Under majority with agenda control, the hegemon extracts surplus through coalition arithmetic. Under consensus, it extracts through screening and persuasion: the unanimity requirement creates a bargaining cutoff at which weak proposers switch to more favorable treatment of the hegemon, and Bayesian Persuasion amplifies that advantage. The institutional choice is between modes of power. Formal equality may be the price of making screening and persuasion more effective than agenda control.

## Relation to existing explanations

The argument relates to two existing accounts.

@steinberg2002shadow argues that consensus does not eliminate power in the WTO; powerful states retain influence through informal agenda setting and control over drafting. That observation corrects naive accounts of formal equality, but it addresses a different question: how power operates within an existing consensus institution. This paper asks why a hegemon would choose such an institution. The answer is that consensus changes the technology of extraction. It substitutes screening and persuasion for coalition exclusion.

The WTO's Green Room process illustrates the distinction. Small-group negotiations among major trading powers may shape which proposals reach the floor, but under consensus any member can still block adoption at the plenary stage. Agenda influence does not restore the coalition-exclusion logic of majority rule. If powerful states extract surplus under consensus, the channel must run through beliefs and screening, not coalition formation. The model imposes a sharper discipline on this point: if unanimity is genuinely binding, proposal control alone does not suffice to restore the majority-style extraction logic. The hegemon's leverage must operate through the screening structure, not through coalition arithmetic.

@maggi2006selfenforcing study voting rule choice in a self-enforcing environment with symmetric states. The present paper focuses on distribution under asymmetric information. The mechanism depends on screening, proposal power, and persuasion, placing the argument within distributive theories of institutional design rather than self-enforcement models. Superficially similar institutions may solve different political problems: consensus supports self-enforcement in some settings and informational extraction in others.

## Commitment and institutional infrastructure

Bayesian Persuasion requires commitment to an information structure. In domestic politics, that commitment is often taken as given. In international politics, it is not [@crawford1982strategic]. Rules, procedures, and structured multilateral practices may serve as commitment devices that make informational influence credible. On this view, multilateralism is part of the institutional technology through which hegemonic informational power operates.

## Scope conditions

The mechanism should be most relevant where the capacity to evaluate agreements is costly and unevenly distributed. In multilateral trade negotiations, technical expertise requires sustained investment in specialized personnel and analytic infrastructure. States with large permanent delegations are better positioned to assess the value of cooperation and to shape how that value is understood by others. States with more limited resources form beliefs on less precise information. This asymmetry provides an empirical foundation for the hegemon's informational advantage.

When weaker states invest in independent analytical capacity, the informational asymmetry narrows and the returns to persuasion decline. The model implies that the value of consensus to a hegemon should covary with the distribution of technical expertise across the membership.
