# Parecer de Contribution (Framework Edmans)

**Paper**: "Informational Power and Institutional Design: When a Hegemon May Choose Consensus"
**Author**: Manoel Galdino (USP)
**Version**: v4 (formal_model_v4.Rmd)
**Date**: 2026-04-26
**Reviewer**: Senior editor evaluation, Edmans (2025) framework adapted for political science
**Context**: Author is deciding whether to remove Bayesian Persuasion from the body and keep only screening as the central mechanism.

---

## Score: 7.0 / 10

This is a solid paper with a genuinely novel mechanism, but the contribution is diluted by trying to serve two audiences simultaneously (formal theory / information design and IR / institutional design). The core insight---that unanimity creates screening rents from informational asymmetry that majority eliminates---is strong enough to carry the paper. The BP layer adds formal elegance but introduces vulnerabilities that weaken rather than strengthen the contribution for the target journals (JoP, AJPS).

---

## Resumo da contribuicao alegada

The paper claims that consensus (unanimity) in international organizations is not a concession to weaker states but an institutional technology of power. The mechanism has two layers: (1) under unanimity, weak states must include the hegemon without knowing the value of cooperation, creating a screening problem that generates a discrete jump in the hegemon's payoff---a rent absent under majority rule; (2) Bayesian persuasion exploits the non-convexity created by this jump, extending the hegemon's advantage to priors where the institution would not otherwise form. The institutional comparison has a single-crossing structure: above a threshold prior, unanimity dominates; below it, majority can dominate through easier participation.

## Avaliacao por dimensao

### Novidade [Forte]

The screening mechanism itself is genuinely novel. No prior paper has identified that unanimity creates a screening problem (weak proposers choosing between aggressive and conservative offers to a privately-informed pivotal player) that generates a discrete payoff jump absent under majority rule. This is a real contribution. The reader updates beliefs significantly: the conventional wisdom in IR is that consensus constrains the powerful (Keohane 1984, Ikenberry 2001) or masks informal power (Steinberg 2002). Neither account identifies a mechanism through which consensus directly amplifies informational advantage. The paper does.

The link to Bayesian persuasion adds a second layer of novelty but in a more derivative way. The application of concavification to exploit a non-convexity generated by screening is methodologically sound but follows the standard Kamenica-Gentzkow logic. The novelty here is in the application, not the technique. More importantly, as the author's own notes acknowledge (notes/2026-04-26_bp_vs_screening_decisao.md), BP amplification equals zero in the paper's own Example 2 across the entire unanimity entry set. This means the headline mechanism---the screening jump---does the work in the most natural parameterization, and BP is a second-order extension that operates only under specific parameter configurations (low c, moderate r).

**Assessment**: The screening mechanism alone is strongly novel. BP adds marginal novelty for the IR audience but substantial novelty for a formal theory audience (the interaction of screening non-convexity and concavification in a voting-rule comparison is new). For JoP/AJPS, the screening insight is the main contribution.

### Importancia [Adequada]

The question "why would a hegemon choose consensus?" is important for IR. It touches the design of major international organizations (GATT/WTO, UN Security Council procedures, regional trade agreements). A survey of institutional design in IOs would mention this paper's answer. A policymaker designing a new international institution would find the screening logic relevant: it says that giving all members a veto can benefit the most informed player, not just constrain them.

However, the importance is tempered by three factors:

1. **Narrow empirical domain**: The mechanism requires informational asymmetry about the value of cooperation, a moderately valuable bilateral alternative for the hegemon, costly entry, and pivotal inclusion under unanimity. The paper acknowledges these scope conditions but does not calibrate how many real-world institutions satisfy all of them simultaneously. The GATT/WTO fits, but the paper does not systematically examine other cases.

2. **Distributional implications underexplored**: The paper establishes that H gains and W loses under unanimity (budget identity + Lemma 1), but never computes W's ex ante payoff under the hegemon's optimal institution. This matters because "consensus as a technology of power" implies distributional consequences that the paper does not fully trace through. A survey would note this gap.

3. **The competitive landscape**: The paper sits near Bardhi and Guo (2018) on multi-receiver persuasion under unanimity and Kim, Kim, and Van Weelden (2025) on BP in bilateral veto bargaining. The differentiation is compressed to a single sentence in the introduction. A referee at a top journal will ask: does the screening non-convexity already appear in those papers? If not, what structural feature of this model generates it? The paper needs to answer this more explicitly.

**Assessment**: The question is important, the answer is substantively relevant, and the mechanism is crisp. But the importance is somewhat constrained by the scope conditions and the underexplored distributional side.

### Adequacao ao escopo [Adequada]

The paper fits JoP well. The references are predominantly from political science and IR: Baron and Ferejohn (1989), Kalandrakis (2006), Eraslan and Evdokimov (2019), Koremenos et al. (2001), Steinberg (2002), Keohane (1984), Ikenberry (2001), Maggi and Morelli (2006), Fearon (1995). The formal theory is standard for JoP (which publishes legislative bargaining models regularly). The substantive framing---institutional choice in IOs---is squarely within the journal's scope.

The Bayesian persuasion component pulls the paper slightly toward formal theory journals (JET, TE, GEB). This is not disqualifying---JoP has published papers using BP (Schnakenberg 2015, 2017)---but it raises the bar for accessibility. The concavification apparatus is well-known in economics but less so in political science. The paper handles this reasonably well by describing BP in substantive terms, but the Section 6 treatment still requires the reader to understand concave envelopes, net-gain functions, and the logic of information design.

For AJPS, the paper would need to lean more heavily on the empirical implications and less on the formal machinery. For JoP, the current balance is appropriate.

**Assessment**: Well-suited for JoP. The BP component is at the boundary of what the audience expects, but not outside it.

### Generalizabilidade [Limitada]

This is the paper's weakest dimension. Several structural features limit generalizability:

1. **Binary type space**: The discrete jump at the screening cutoff is the engine of the mechanism. With continuous types, the offer schedule would generically be smooth, and the specific non-convexity that BP exploits would vanish. The paper acknowledges this in the conclusion and cites Dworczak and Martini (2019) for bunching regions under continuous types, but this is a conjecture, not a result. The defense that "states evaluate cooperation in terms of qualitatively distinct regimes" is asserted without evidence.

2. **K > 2 fragility**: The paper's own Appendix C reveals that the parametric region where unanimity dominates shrinks with K. The coarse review correctly identified this as a structural vulnerability: nothing rules out alpha_K* converging to zero as K grows. The paper is forthright about this ("the binary model is the most favorable case for the mechanism"), which is commendable, but it means the result is established only for the simplest possible information structure.

3. **Two-round bargaining**: The screening mechanism depends on type theta=1 rejecting in R1 and perfectly revealing the state in R2. With T > 2 rounds, rejection would not fully reveal the type, and the screening dynamics would change. The paper argues that the two-round structure is a robustness check (the motivating example shows the mechanism in one round), but the R1 screening cutoff being independent of alpha is a feature of the specific two-round structure that may not survive richer protocols.

4. **All-or-nothing entry**: The simplification that all N-1 weak states must enter simultaneously avoids modeling coalition formation at the entry stage. This is a reasonable simplification, but it means the model cannot address partial membership, which is common in real IOs.

5. **Symmetric weak states**: All weak states are identical, ruling out middle powers with heterogeneous outside options. The paper flags this as a future extension.

**Assessment**: The mechanism is sharp within its setting but structurally fragile. The binary type space is doing heavy lifting. The paper is honest about limitations, which helps, but a referee may conclude that the result is a possibility theorem for a specific class of environments rather than a general theory.

### Trade-offs [Parcial]

The paper partially addresses trade-offs:

**What is covered**:
- H gains vs. W loses under unanimity: The budget identity implies V_W(mu, U) < V_W(mu, M), and this is stated explicitly in the text after Lemma 1.
- Entry is harder under unanimity (tau(U) > tau(M)): the paper discusses this as the channel through which majority can dominate.
- The alpha* threshold: below it, unanimity dominates conditionally; above it, majority can dominate near mu = 1.
- The Remark on robustness beyond alpha* is well done, showing that the mechanism fails only near certainty.

**What is missing**:
- W's ex ante payoff under H's optimal institution with persuasion: If BP induces W to enter at posteriors where V_W barely exceeds c, W may be strictly worse off ex ante. This is never computed.
- Why W would accept unanimity: The paper says "W does not choose the rule; H chooses." But in many real IOs, rule choice is itself a negotiated outcome. If W always prefers majority, why does unanimity emerge? The paper's answer ("the veto protects W against exclusion") is in the decision notes but not in the paper itself. This is actually a strong point that should be developed: W accepts unanimity because the veto provides insurance against exclusion, but this same insurance creates the screening opportunity for H. This irony is the heart of the mechanism and deserves explicit treatment.
- Welfare analysis: Is unanimity efficient or merely redistributive? Does total surplus increase or decrease? The budget check (A.6) shows surplus destruction on the aggressive branch, suggesting unanimity can be inefficient. This is mentioned but not quantified.

**Assessment**: The distributive conflict is acknowledged but not fully developed. The paper would benefit from a paragraph explicitly computing W's payoff under the optimal institution and discussing welfare implications.

### Hipoteses [Claras e direcionais]

The hypotheses are exceptionally clear:

1. **Lemma 1** (Conditional dominance): alpha < alpha* iff unanimity gives H strictly higher payoff at every belief. Sharp, biconditional, closed-form threshold.
2. **Theorem 1** (Dominance): For every prior in the unanimity entry set, H strictly prefers unanimity with persuasion. Pointwise, no oscillation.
3. **Theorem 2** (Single-crossing): The set of priors where unanimity dominates is an upper interval. Unique threshold p*.

The mechanism is directional and testable in principle: consensus should matter most where informational asymmetry is consequential (complex regulatory negotiations), where the hegemon's bilateral alternatives are moderate (alpha < alpha*), and where bargaining is patient (high beta). The GATT/WTO discussion generates cross-issue predictions (Section 8.1): the mechanism should be strongest in services, IP, investment rules, and weakest in simple tariff cuts. The PTA/Bhagwati prediction (increasing alpha weakens the mechanism) is distinctive.

**Assessment**: The formal hypotheses are among the clearest I have seen in a formal IR theory paper. The comparative statics are rich and generate testable predictions. This is a significant strength.

## How would the contribution change WITHOUT BP?

This is the central question the author faces. My assessment:

### What is GAINED by removing BP from the body

1. **Elimination of the commitment vulnerability (CR3)**: The coarse review correctly identified the BP commitment assumption as the paper's most questionable behavioral assumption. Without BP in the body, the paper requires only standard game-theoretic assumptions: PBE, sequential rationality, Bayes' rule. No commitment to information structures. This makes the paper essentially bulletproof on the behavioral assumptions front.

2. **Cleaner narrative**: The paper's story becomes: "Unanimity creates screening rents from informational asymmetry. Majority eliminates them. The hegemon prefers unanimity whenever the institution is viable." This is a one-sentence mechanism. With BP, the story becomes: "Unanimity creates screening rents, and BP can exploit the non-convexity they generate to extend the advantage to lower priors." The second sentence adds complexity without adding much substance for most parameterizations.

3. **Resolution of the BP amplification = 0 problem**: The paper's own Example 2 shows zero BP amplification in the entire entry set. This is embarrassing for a paper that features BP as a co-protagonist. Removing BP from the body eliminates the need to explain why the second mechanism contributes nothing in the central example.

4. **Better journal fit**: JoP and AJPS readers are comfortable with legislative bargaining and screening. Concavification and information design are less familiar. Removing BP lowers the technical barrier to entry.

5. **Sharper scope conditions**: Without BP, the paper's result is: H prefers unanimity for all p in E_U. The scope condition is simply "entry is feasible under unanimity." With BP, the result extends to p > p*, but explaining p* requires the entire concavification apparatus.

### What is LOST by removing BP from the body

1. **The extension to priors below tau(U)**: BP allows H to induce entry at priors where the institution would not otherwise form under unanimity. Without BP, majority dominates at any prior where entry occurs only under majority. This narrows the domain of unanimity dominance.

2. **The asymmetry result**: "BP is useful under unanimity but useless under majority" (because v(mu, M) is affine) is a beautiful formal observation. It captures something real: the voting rule determines the returns to information manipulation. Losing this is a genuine intellectual cost.

3. **Theoretical completeness**: The paper currently characterizes the hegemon's optimal strategy over the full parameter space. Without BP, the paper leaves open what happens for p < tau(U). A referee might ask: "You've shown H prefers unanimity conditional on entry, but what if H can also manipulate beliefs? Doesn't this change the comparison?" The paper would need to either ignore this question or address it in an appendix.

4. **Differentiation from simpler models**: With BP, the paper sits at the intersection of legislative bargaining, information design, and IO theory---a unique position. Without BP, it becomes a legislative bargaining paper with an IO application. Still good, but less distinctive.

5. **The single-crossing theorem (Theorem 2)**: This is formally elegant and would be lost from the body. The result that the institutional ranking never oscillates is clean and memorable.

### Net assessment

**Removing BP from the body and keeping it as an appendix extension is likely the right move for JoP/AJPS.** Here is why:

The contribution score AS IS (with BP as co-protagonist) is 7.0/10. The contribution score WITHOUT BP in the body (screening only, BP in appendix) would be approximately **7.5/10** for JoP. The reason is that the screening mechanism alone is a strong, clean contribution, and BP introduces vulnerabilities (commitment, zero amplification in the main example, accessibility) that currently drag the score down. Moving BP to an appendix ("if the hegemon can also design information structures, the advantage extends to lower priors") preserves the intellectual contribution while removing the vulnerabilities from the referee's primary evaluation.

However, the contribution would be approximately **6.5/10** for a formal theory journal (JET, TE) without BP, because the screening mechanism alone, while novel in the IR context, is a relatively standard observation in mechanism design: a principal with private information interacting with an uninformed agent under a participation constraint generates screening rents. The BP layer is what makes the paper interesting to formal theorists.

**Recommendation**: For JoP, Option B (BP subordinated to screening) or Option A (BP in appendix) is preferred. The paper should be structured as: (1) Screening mechanism (Lemma 1) as the central result; (2) Institutional comparison conditional on entry (Theorem 1); (3) BP as a one-paragraph extension or appendix section that shows the advantage extends to lower priors. If the author prefers a formal theory journal, keep BP in the body but address the commitment vulnerability seriously (show the qualitative result survives under cheap talk or verifiable disclosure).

## Veredicto geral sobre contribution

The paper identifies a genuinely novel mechanism linking voting rules to informational power in international organizations. The screening insight---that unanimity forces weak states to include the hegemon under uncertainty, creating rents that majority eliminates---is the paper's strongest contribution. It is clean, sharp, and has clear comparative statics with testable implications. The formal results are correct and well-organized; the single-crossing structure is elegant.

The contribution is currently diluted by the dual-mechanism narrative. Bayesian persuasion adds formal completeness but introduces the commitment assumption (CR3), the zero-amplification problem in the main example (CR8), and accessibility costs for the JoP audience. For the author's target journals, the screening mechanism alone carries the paper.

The main limitations are generalizability (binary types, K > 2 fragility, two-round bargaining) and the partially developed distributional analysis (W's perspective, welfare). These are standard concerns for formal theory papers and do not disqualify the contribution, but they should be addressed more explicitly.

Overall, this is a paper with a clear, novel, and substantively important mechanism that is somewhat obscured by the dual-mechanism framing. Streamlining around screening would raise the contribution score.

## Sugestoes construtivas

1. **Elevate the irony**: The paper's most powerful insight is buried in the decision notes: W accepts unanimity because the veto protects against exclusion, but this protection creates the screening opportunity that benefits H. "Formal equality is the mechanism of extraction." This should be in the introduction, not just implied by the model. It is the paper's unique rhetorical contribution to the IO design literature.

2. **Compute W's ex ante payoff explicitly**: For the Example 2 parameters, compute V_W under H's optimal institution under both rules. Show the distributional conflict quantitatively. This addresses the coarse review's CR7 concern and strengthens the "technology of power" framing.

3. **Develop the no-BP version of the institutional comparison**: State clearly: "Conditional on entry, H strictly prefers unanimity (Lemma 1). Entry is harder under unanimity (tau(U) > tau(M)). Without persuasion, the comparison is ambiguous: unanimity dominates when both rules induce entry; majority dominates when only it can." This is a clean, complete result that does not require BP. Then add: "Bayesian persuasion resolves the ambiguity in favor of unanimity at all priors above p*" as an extension.

4. **Address the K > 2 fragility more constructively**: Rather than just acknowledging that the binary case is most favorable, argue positively for why binary types are the right abstraction. In trade negotiations, the key uncertainty is often "will this round produce a deal or not?"---qualitatively distinct regimes, not marginal variations. This maps naturally to K = 2. The continuous-type case is theoretically interesting but may not be the empirically relevant one.

5. **Expand differentiation from Bardhi-Guo and Kim-Kim-Van Weelden**: Two to three paragraphs explaining which structural features (legislative bargaining protocol, asymmetric outside options, entry stage, institutional comparison) generate results absent in those papers. This is essential for a top-journal submission.

6. **Add a calibrated comparative static on p***: Show how p* responds to alpha, r, N, c. Map early GATT (small N, large informational asymmetry) to one parameter region and Doha Round (large N, narrower asymmetry) to another. This gives the formal apparatus empirical bite.

7. **Discuss the two-round limitation explicitly**: Acknowledge that the perfect revelation in R2 depends on the terminal-round structure. Argue that with T > 2 rounds, screening would still generate overpayment (a weak proposer facing a privately-informed pivotal player must offer at least the high type's reservation value to guarantee acceptance), even if the specific functional form changes. The qualitative mechanism---uncertainty about the hegemon's type creates a cost for the proposer---is not an artifact of T = 2.

8. **For the BP commitment assumption (if BP stays in the body)**: Show that the qualitative institutional comparison---unanimity generates screening that majority does not---holds under cheap talk or verifiable disclosure. If the comparison survives without commitment, BP is a sharpening device, not a load-bearing assumption. This would neutralize CR3 entirely.
