## Section 1: Literature Review (lines 61–70)

### Paragraph 1 (APPROVED)

**ORIGINAL:**

> The paper contributes to the literature on institutional design in international organizations. The dominant explanations for consensus can be grouped into three categories. Consensus as self-binding: powerful states accept formal constraints to make credible commitments, encouraging participation by weaker partners [@keohane1984after; @ikenberry2001after]. Consensus as irrelevant: informal agenda power, control over drafting, and asymmetric resources allow powerful states to shape outcomes regardless of the formal voting rule [@steinberg2002shadow; @stone2011controlling; @gruber2000ruling]. Consensus as rational design: voting rules are chosen to match the cooperation problem an organization faces [@koremenos2001rational]. @gould2016consensus provides the most comprehensive empirical account, documenting the prevalence and variation of consensus across international organizations. This paper offers a distinct account: consensus as a technology that activates informational power. The mechanism does not require informal influence, does not serve as self-binding, and is not chosen to solve commitment problems. It is preferred because it makes the hegemon's private information more valuable.

**APPROVED VERSION:**

> The dominant explanations for consensus in international organizations fall into two categories, each missing a different piece of the puzzle. Self-binding accounts hold that powerful states accept formal constraints to make credible commitments, encouraging weaker partners to participate [@keohane1984after; @ikenberry2001after]. These accounts explain why consensus sustains cooperation, but not why it gives the hegemon a distributive advantage. Informal power accounts imply that agenda power, control over drafting, and asymmetric resources let powerful states shape outcomes regardless of the formal rule [@steinberg2002shadow; @stone2011controlling; @gruber2000ruling]. They cannot explain why formal rules vary systematically across organizations. @gould2016consensus documents the prevalence and variation of consensus comprehensively. This paper identifies a mechanism absent from existing accounts: consensus activates informational power. The hegemon's private information becomes more valuable under unanimity than under majority.

### Paragraph 3 (APPROVED — restructured: Koremenos + Kim; Bardhi & Guo → footnote)

**ORIGINAL:**

> The paper is most closely related to formal work on information in voting and bargaining. @bardhi2018modes study information design under unanimity, showing how a persuader can manipulate the beliefs of multiple receivers who must unanimously agree. Their framework does not include bargaining over a divisible surplus, endogenous screening by the uninformed side, or a comparison between voting rules. @kim2025persuasion study persuasion in veto bargaining, analyzing how an information designer augments agenda-setting power when a proposer faces a veto player. Their model is bilateral and takes the veto rule as given. Neither paper addresses the question that is central here: how the voting rule itself determines whether screening occurs. In both, information design is the instrument; in the present paper, the voting rule is the instrument, and screening is the endogenous consequence of pivotality under uncertainty.

**APPROVED VERSION:**

> @koremenos2001rational conjecture that powerful states seek asymmetric control (V2) but that uncertainty increases demand for unanimity as insurance for risk-averse states (V3). These conjectures are not formally derived, and when both forces are present they pull in opposite directions without resolution. The present model formalizes this interaction: all players are risk-neutral, and unanimity benefits the hegemon through screening, not insurance. Uncertainty makes the hegemon's private information valuable because weak proposers cannot distinguish types, a mechanism that is incentive-compatible and does not require risk aversion.^[For information design under unanimity without bargaining, see @bardhi2018modes.] @kim2025persuasion study persuasion in veto bargaining, analyzing how an information designer augments agenda-setting power when a proposer faces a veto player. Their model is bilateral and takes the veto rule as given. Neither addresses how the voting rule itself determines whether screening occurs.

### NEW: "Why proportional outside options?" (absorbs Definition 1 footnote 2) (APPROVED)

**NEW SCOPE PARAGRAPH (insert after "Why symmetric proposal rights?"):**

> **Why proportional outside options?** The hegemon's disagreement payoff $d_H = \alpha V(\theta)$ scales with the value of multilateral cooperation: the same factors that make the multilateral deal valuable also improve the hegemon's bilateral fallback. This proportional form separates informational power (which depends on $\theta$ being unknown to weak states) from pure bargaining power (which depends on $\alpha$). The restriction $\alpha < 1/r$ ensures the bilateral alternative remains strictly dominated by multilateral cooperation in every state.

### "What exactly is being compared?" (line 610) (APPROVED)

**APPROVED VERSION:**

> **What exactly is being compared?** The comparison is between unanimity and symmetric simple majority. Majority rule here does not grant the hegemon agenda control, weighted voting, or privileged coalition-building capacity. This choice isolates the effect of the voting rule on information use, holding all other institutional features constant. Majority rules in international organizations often come bundled with weighted voting or asymmetric proposal rights (as in the IMF or World Bank), which would independently benefit the hegemon. Remark \ref{rem:weighted} explores what happens when the hegemon's voting weight increases: the mechanism activates whenever coalitions strategically include the hegemon, whether or not formal rules require its vote.

### "Why all-or-nothing entry?" (line 614) (APPROVED — absorbs Definition 1 footnote 4)

**APPROVED VERSION:**

> **Why all-or-nothing entry?** All-or-nothing entry isolates the screening mechanism by suppressing dynamics that sequential accession would introduce: free-riding, learning from early joiners, and collective action problems among weak states. The parameter $N$ is the size of the institution the hegemon proposes to create, not the total number of states in the international system; the hegemon invites a specific group of $N-1$ weak states, and their decision is whether to join this particular institution. This simplification fits organizations where states negotiate membership as a package (founding moments like the GATT in 1947, the WTO in 1995, or new regulatory bodies) rather than organizations with rolling accession. With sequential entry, multiple mechanisms would operate in opposing directions, and the net effect on the hegemon's institutional preference would depend on their relative magnitudes. The model focuses on the screening channel alone; characterizing its interaction with sequential entry dynamics remains open.

### Line 709 paragraph (APPROVED)

**APPROVED VERSION:**

> The threshold $\alpha^*$ decreases with $N$ because unanimity requires buying $N-1$ votes, while majority requires only $q-1$. Below $\alpha^*$, unanimity dominates at every prior belief, regardless of how uncertain weak states are about $\theta$. Above $\alpha^*$, beliefs start to matter, but majority gains an advantage only near $\mu = 1$, where weak states nearly know the hegemon's type and informational asymmetry vanishes. Over most of the belief space, unanimity continues to dominate. As Remark \ref{rem:mu_bar} shows, increasing $\alpha$ from $0.05$ to $0.49$ for $N = 164$ shifts the frontier only from $\bar\mu = 0.87$ to $0.71$. An 18-fold increase in the outside option compresses the unanimity-dominance region by less than 20\%. Figure \@ref(fig:heatmap-alpha-mu) illustrates: even in the $N = 164$ panels, the blue region (where unanimity gives the hegemon a higher conditional payoff) covers most of the $(\alpha, \mu)$ plane, while the red region always clusters near certainty.
